Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners

Radford et al., OpenAI · OpenAI 2019 · openai.com

Trained on one dumb objective — predict the next word across 40GB of internet — it started translating, summarizing, and answering questions anyway. Nobody trained it to do any of that.

the aha · figure 1, redrawn as a schematic

Four tasks it was never taught

One objective — predict the next token across 40GB of web text — and four tasks nobody trained appear anyway, each climbing as the model grows from 117M to 1.5B parameters. The same model set state of the art on 7 of 8 language-modeling datasets zero-shot, while still underfitting its training set. It's like discovering that someone hired only to finish other people's sentences had — in order to get genuinely good at that across the whole internet — quietly learned to translate, summarize, and answer trivia, because you cannot reliably finish humanity's sentences without those skills.

one objective, many tasks · watch it move

step 01 / 04

One dumb objective

Predict the next token on 40GB of web text. That's the entire training signal — no labels for translation, summarisation, or question answering. The bet: those tasks are latent in human writing, so modelling the writing well means learning the tasks inside it.

step 02 / 04

Four tasks, climbing with size

Reading comprehension, translation, summarisation, question answering — each panel shows zero-shot performance rising as the model grows from 117M to 1.5B parameters. No task-specific training anywhere on these curves. The line just goes up as the model gets bigger.

step 03 / 04

The dashed lines are the specialists

Each dashed line is a dedicated supervised system built for that one task. GPT-2 doesn't beat them — but the gap narrows as it scales, from a general model that was never told these tasks existed.

step 04 / 04

The bet this started

The capabilities you didn't build are already in the model. That reframed the whole job: not 'how do I train a model for task X' but 'how do I elicit task X from a general one.' Every prompt you write is that question, and GPT-3 proved the bet at scale two papers from here.

what it changed

From training to eliciting

This is where the modern builder's default question comes from: not "how do I train a model for task X" but "how do I elicit task X from a general model that already learned it." Capabilities you didn't build are already in the model — prompts, examples, and fine-tuning are extraction techniques, not creation. The release story matters too: the staged rollout and the "too dangerous to release" episode around the 1.5B weights started the release-norms playbook every lab still follows.

honest limits

Trained to continue, not to chat

The misconception: "GPT-2 was trained to chat or follow instructions." It was trained to do exactly one thing: continue text. Tasks show up because demonstrations of tasks occur naturally in web text — "translated into French: …" appears often enough that predicting what comes next is translation. Instruction-following didn't exist until RLHF, three years later.

And the dashed lines in the figure are load-bearing: on these four tasks, even the 1.5B model still sat below the dedicated supervised systems of the day. Figure 1 is a thesis about slopes, not a victory over baselines — the visual bet that the lines keep climbing, which GPT-3 proved at scale a year later.
