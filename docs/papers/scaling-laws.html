Capability got a price list

step 01 / 04

Straight lines for eight orders of magnitude

Figure 1 plots test loss against model size, data, and compute on log–log axes, and the points fall on ruler-straight power laws — L(N) = (Nc/N)0.076 for model size — holding across 8 orders of magnitude. Nobody needs to parse the exponent: the straight lines are the message. Run small, cheap experiments, put a ruler through the points, and read off what a 1,000× bigger run will deliver — before writing the check.

step 02 / 04

Spend the budget

The widget's default mode is the industry's actual planning tool. Pick a compute budget C and the frontier answers back: the optimal model size Nopt = √(C/120), the tokens to feed it (20 per parameter), the loss you land at, and the GPU-rental bill at 2026 prices. The vintage line tells you which year of capability you just bought — and the verdict line tells you whether a frontier lab already passed you.

step 03 / 04

Allocate it yourself

Mode B splits the decision the paper left half-answered: with C ≈ 6·N·D, how much goes to parameters and how much to tokens? Kaplan's own advice — grow the model, barely grow the data — turned out wrong. Chinchilla (2022) redid the fits: scale both together, about 20 tokens per parameter. The widget runs the 2022-corrected constants — the caveats drawer explains why.

step 04 / 04

Now make GPT-3's mistake yourself

Press the preset: 175B parameters on 300B tokens — 1.7 tokens per parameter, exactly how GPT-3 was trained. Your loss reads 2.002 nats; the frontier at the very same spend reads 1.961, from a model a third the size fed ~1T tokens. Same money, worse model. Chinchilla then made the point in production — 70B params on 1.4T tokens landed at 1.937, beating GPT-3's configuration outright — and now you've made OpenAI's 2020 allocation error with your own hands.

the fine print — ten caveats
  1. Kaplan and Chinchilla contradict each other on the frontier: Kaplan said N ∝ C0.73 — grow the model, barely grow the data, which is why GPT-3 was 175B params on 300B tokens. Chinchilla corrected it to N ∝ C0.5 with ~20 tokens/param. The causes have since been identified (Pearce & Song 2024, arXiv:2406.12907): Kaplan counted non-embedding parameters only, used a learning-rate schedule not tuned to the token budget, and fit at much smaller scales. The widget follows Chinchilla.
  2. The two papers' loss values are not on the same scale: different datasets (WebText2 vs MassiveText) and different tokenizers, so nats/token differ. Never plot both fits on one y-axis without saying the Kaplan curve is illustrative shape only.
  3. Chinchilla's own published Approach-3 constants are internally inconsistent: plugged into the analytic frontier they imply ~70–90 tokens/param, not the paper's headline 20. Epoch AI's replication (Besiroglu et al. 2024, arXiv:2404.10102) found the fit non-reproducible and re-fit constants that restore ~20:1. The widget uses the parametric form for loss but the 20:1 rule for the frontier.
  4. These laws describe pretraining loss only. Post-training (RLHF, reasoning RL à la DeepSeek-R1) and inference-time compute (chain-of-thought) move capability without moving pretraining FLOPs — most of what changed between 2023 and 2025 lives there and is invisible on this chart.
  5. Loss → capability is nonlinear: a 0.05-nat drop can unlock qualitatively new behavior, or nothing. The capability labels on the anchors are honest vibes, not measurements; specific downstream skills can appear abruptly ("emergence") even though loss falls smoothly.
  6. C ≈ 6·N·D is an approximation: it ignores attention FLOPs (which matter at long context), and for MoE models you must use active parameters — GPT-4 is ~280B active, not 1.8T total; using total overestimates compute 6×.
  7. The dollar axis is compute-rental-only at mid-2026 neocloud prices (~$2.50/H100-hr, 40% MFU): it excludes salaries, failed runs, ablation experiments, data acquisition, and networking — real all-in budgets are typically 2–5× the rental number. And prices fell 64–75% from Q4 2024 to 2026, so historical models cost far more on the hardware of their day (GPT-3: ~$4.6M then vs ~$550k of compute now).
  8. Chinchilla-optimal means optimal for a single training run, ignoring inference: if you will serve the model to millions of users, it is rational to overtrain a smaller model past 20:1 (Llama 3.1 405B used ~38 tokens/param; Llama 3 8B used ~1,900). The frontier is a floor on loss per training FLOP, not a business recommendation.
  9. The fits are extrapolations beyond ~1e24 FLOPs: Chinchilla's largest fitted models were ~16B params, so every curve point at GPT-4 scale and beyond extends the power law two-plus orders of magnitude past the data. It has held remarkably well so far, but the chart shades the extrapolated region.
  10. The irreducible term E = 1.69 nats/token is the entropy of the text distribution itself — loss never reaches zero no matter the spend. The curve flattens toward the asymptote, which is the visual argument for why pure pretraining scaling has diminishing returns and the industry pivoted to post-training and inference-time compute.

what it changed

"Wait for the next model" becomes arithmetic

Before this paper, a bigger training run was a bet. After it, capability is a purchasable, forecastable commodity — which is why billion-dollar training runs are capital allocation, not gambles, and why the labs schedule products, GPU purchases, and fundraising rounds against these curves. For a builder the rule is defensive: never build the thing one more order of magnitude erases. If your product's entire value is compensating for a current model weakness, the frontier has a rough price and date for deleting it. The framework survived its own correction — Chinchilla (2022) moved the coefficients, not the conclusion — and it still sets the industry's calendar.

honest limits

A regularity, not physics

The misconception comes in two opposite flavors: "scaling has hit a wall" and "scaling laws guarantee AGI." Both over-read the equation. The laws predict loss on next-token prediction, not specific abilities — and the mapping from a smoothly falling loss curve to capabilities is not in the equation. Individual skills can jump discontinuously while loss glides down, in both exciting and unnerving ways.

The honest analogy is Moore's Law for capability: an empirical regularity, not a law of physics — yet reliable enough that an entire industry has scheduled itself around it for years. And the paper's own authors misread their chart: the widget's "GPT-3's mistake" preset exists because Kaplan's allocation rule stood for two years before Chinchilla showed the same compute buys a better model — smaller, fed more data. Even a law this straight bends people.

build it: $0–30 · a weekend

    Read the paper — Figure 1's three straight lines are the message; nobody needs to parse the exponents. Then note the 2022 Chinchilla correction: scale data with parameters.

    2 hrs · $0

  2. run

    Discover the law yourself: train three or four nanoGPT sizes (0.1M / 1M / 6M params) on the same data, plot final loss vs params on log-log axes, and watch the straight line appear — Kaplan's trends are visible at just 6.3M params, per an open replication with public training curves.

    a weekend (mostly unattended training) · $0–30
