Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition

He, Zhang, Ren & Sun, Microsoft Research · CVPR 2016 · arXiv 1512.03385

A 56-layer network was losing to its own 20-layer version — until two characters of algebra, '+ x', made 152 layers trainable and swept ImageNet.

the aha · the residual block, redrawn

Two characters of algebra

The paradox that motivated it (the paper's Figure 1): a plain 56-layer network had higher training error than a 20-layer one. Not overfitting — the optimizer literally couldn't find the deeper solution, because every layer had to re-derive everything the ones before it knew. Make pass-through the default and depth becomes nearly free: 152 layers, 3.57% top-5 error, first place at ILSVRC 2015. Think of a document passing through 50 editors as tracked changes, instead of each editor retyping the whole thing from memory.

what it changed

You use this every day

The "residual stream" that interpretability work talks about is literally this + x path — a skip connection sits inside every block of every Transformer serving you today. The design lesson generalizes to systems you build now: when quality degrades as you add stages to a pipeline or an agent chain, make pass-through the default and have each stage contribute only a delta, instead of forcing every stage to reconstruct the whole state.

honest limits

Not overfitting, not vanishing gradients

The misconception: "deep nets failed because they had too many parameters (overfitting) or purely because gradients vanished." The paper's own evidence says otherwise: training error itself got worse with depth — an optimization failure. Skip connections fix it by making identity trivially learnable, so extra depth can only help.

The limit: "+ x" buys trainability, not capability. Depth stopped being the bottleneck — two years later the question became what the layers should do, and the answer deleted recurrence entirely.
