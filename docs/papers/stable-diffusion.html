the aha · figure 3, redrawn

Paint on the maquette, not the marble

The arithmetic is checkable on a napkin: a 512×512 image is 786,432 numbers, but most of them are perceptual detail, not meaning. So the model first compresses the image with an autoencoder into a 64×64×4 latent — 16,384 numbers — and runs the entire denoising loop there: roughly 48× less work on every one of ~50 steps, decoding to pixels exactly once at the end. It's a sculptor working on a maquette instead of the full marble block: every iteration happens where changes are cheap, and only the finished form is scaled up. That discount is what moved image generation from a datacenter to a gaming GPU.

the forward road · touch it

step 01 / 04

Start from a photograph

Forward diffusion only ever does one thing: add a little Gaussian noise, on a fixed schedule. Drag the slider and watch it happen to a real photo — the NASA Blue Marble. This direction is free: pure math, no model, no training.

step 02 / 04

Structure dissolves

Halfway along the schedule the planet is still faintly there, drowning. And notice the scale toggle: the whole loop runs in a latent 48× smaller than the pixels — 16,384 numbers instead of 786,432 — which is the entire reason this fits on a consumer GPU.

step 03 / 04

Pure static

At the end the information is not hidden, it is destroyed — indistinguishable from random noise. Generation is learning to walk this exact road backwards, one small denoising step at a time, steered by your prompt.

step 04 / 04

Now break the myth

Shuffle the noise: same photo, a completely different random draw, every time. There is no image lurking in the static waiting to be uncovered. That is why diffusion does not collage training images — it starts from noise and paints, it does not paste.

what it changed

Change the space, then change the strategy

The technical lesson generalizes: the biggest cost wins come from changing the space you compute in, not from micro-optimizing the loop — the same meta-lesson as CLIP's embeddings, one page back. The strategic lesson is just as durable: Stable Diffusion shipped open weights against a closed, better-funded DALL·E 2 and won the ecosystem within months — fine-tunes, LoRAs, ControlNet, an entire tooling economy built by users who had become the R&D department. That playbook has since been rerun by Llama and DeepSeek-R1, and it sits on the table in every AI product strategy discussion you'll be in this year.

honest limits

Denoising, not collage

The misconception: "diffusion models collage pieces of training images." Generation is iterative denoising from pure random noise, steered by the prompt — there is no lookup, no pasting, no source image. Memorization of near-duplicates exists as an edge case, but it is not the mechanism. The noise the process starts from contains none of the original: the information is destroyed, not hidden.

Credit where it's due, too: this paper did not invent diffusion — DDPM (2020) invented the method; this paper made it affordable; the open-weights release made it a movement. And the maquette has limits of its own — the paper says so: the autoencoder trades away fine-grained pixel accuracy by design, so reconstruction becomes the bottleneck for tasks that need pixel-level precision. The latent space is cheap precisely because it isn't pixels.

Build it yourself

build it: $0 · one evening

  1. read

    Read the paper — Figure 3's three zones (pixel space | latent space | conditioning) answer the real question: how does my prompt become an image? The cross-attention arrows are the Transformer page's equation, reused.

    1–2 hrs · $0

  2. run

    Train a DDPM from scratch on MNIST with the Hugging Face diffusion course — a ~2M-param U-Net, ~10 minutes on a small GPU — then sample it and watch pure noise denoise into digits you generated.

    one evening · $0

  3. tweak

    LoRA-fine-tune actual Stable Diffusion on ~20 of your own photos with diffusers' DreamBooth-LoRA scripts.

    a weekend · $0
