Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback

Ouyang et al., OpenAI · NeurIPS 2022 · arXiv 2203.02155

Human raters preferred a 1.3-billion-parameter model over the 175-billion-parameter GPT-3 — a hundred times smaller, and better, because three training steps taught it to actually try to help.

the aha · the three-step pipeline, redrawn

Three steps teach it to try

A raw language model completes text — nothing in pretraining makes it try to help you. The pipeline bolts that on in three steps: humans write good answers and the model imitates them; humans rank the model's own outputs and a second model learns that preference; then PPO tunes the language model to maximize the learned score. This is the step that turned the GPT-3 curiosity into the assistants everyone now uses. And the abstract's first punch: human labelers preferred the 1.3B InstructGPT over the 175B GPT-3 — 100× fewer parameters, and people liked it more. Media training for a brilliant but rambling expert: no new facts, just answering the question actually asked, in the expected format, and declining appropriately instead of freewheeling.

what it changed

You are feeling the reward model

Model quality as users experience it is substantially post-training, not scale — which reorders your options: preference-tuning on your task can beat paying for a bigger model. It also explains why models from different vendors feel different despite similar pretraining — the "personality" is the reward model showing through. And it tells you what to test: sycophancy, over-hedging, and refusal style are RLHF artifacts, not facts about the underlying model — build evals for them explicitly.

honest limits

Steering, not capability

The misconception: "RLHF makes models smarter." It adds essentially no knowledge. What it reshapes is behavior — instruction-following, format, tone, when to refuse — and the paper is candid that even this has a price: an "alignment tax," measured regressions on some public benchmarks that the authors then work to mitigate. RLHF is steering, not capability.

The analogy runs all the way down: the media-trained expert learns no new facts, and sometimes comes out measurably blander. When a preference-tuned model loses ground on a task the base model handled, that isn't mysterious — it's the trade-off the paper named and measured.

Build it yourself

build it: $0 · one evening

  1. read

    Read the paper — the three-box pipeline (SFT → reward model → PPO) is the most-redrawn diagram in the industry, and the 1.3B-beats-175B result is the abstract's first punch.

    1–2 hrs · $0

  2. run

    Turn a raw base model into an assistant with your own hands: SFT then DPO on Qwen2.5-0.5B with TRL and UltraFeedback preference pairs, on a free Colab T4 — then chat with the before/after checkpoints and feel the alignment layer.

    one evening · $0

  3. train

    Add PPO with TRL's PPOTrainer for the historically faithful version — or walk the author's tiny-llm-pipeline, which runs this same SFT→DPO alignment stage on a from-scratch tiny GPT.

    a weekend · $0

It worked — powered by tens of thousands of human judgments nobody could read, audit, or argue with. Anthropic's counter-proposal: what if the values were just written down?
