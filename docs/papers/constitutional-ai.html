Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback

Bai et al., Anthropic · arXiv 2022 · arXiv 2212.08073

Anthropic swapped tens of thousands of human harmlessness labels for 16 plain-English principles the model applies to itself — and got a model that was less harmful without refusing more.

the aha · the two-phase pipeline, redrawn

The rulebook becomes the dataset

The previous paper on this timeline taught a model manners with tens of thousands of human preference labels. This one keeps that pipeline's shape and swaps the harmlessness labelers out for 16 written principles. Phase 1: the model answers red-team prompts, critiques its own answers against the principles, revises, and is fine-tuned on the revisions. Phase 2: an AI — not a person — compares pairs of responses against the same principles; those judgments train a preference model, and RL runs against it. There is no math anywhere in the loop — the values are ordinary sentences. It's a written editorial policy applied by a junior editor (the model itself) to every draft: humans still author the policy, and unlike a pile of opaque thumbs-up/down labels, you can read it and argue with a specific clause.

step 01 / 04

The labeling problem

The standard way to make a model harmless was tens of thousands of human labels — is this response OK or not. Slow, expensive, opaque, and inconsistent between labelers. You can't read it, argue with it, or diff it.

step 02 / 04

Phase 1: the model revises itself

Give the model a short written constitution and it critiques its own harmful answers against those principles, then rewrites them — and you fine-tune on the revisions. The principles are plain, readable English, shown right here from the paper's appendix.

step 03 / 04

Phase 2: AI feedback

An AI compares response pairs against the constitution; those judgments train a preference model, and reinforcement learning optimises against it. Humans still author the constitution and still provide every helpfulness label — what got automated is applying the rules at scale, not choosing them.

step 04 / 04

The practice

Per the paper, the result was less harmful without refusing more. The pattern — supervise a model with another model plus explicit written rules — is now everywhere: LLM-as-judge, self-critique loops, policy-in-the-prompt. Write your agent guardrails as testable principles you can read, audit, and diff.

what it changed

Models now supervise models

Supervising a model with another model plus explicit written rules is now the standard pattern — LLM-as-judge, self-critique loops, and policy-in-the-prompt all descend from this paper. If you're building agent guardrails, write them as testable natural-language principles instead of accumulating one-off human labels: a constitution scales, you can audit it, and you can diff it like code. It also decodes vendor differences — a model's refusal style is a policy-document choice, not an inevitability of the technology. This paper is why Claude behaves and refuses the way it does: its values are inspectable text.

honest limits

Humans still hold the pen

The misconception: "Constitutional AI means no humans — the AI picks its own values." Humans wrote the constitution, and humans still provide every helpfulness label; what got automated is the application of the rules at scale, not the choice of them. And the safety was not purchased with refusals: per the paper's Elo results (Figure 2), RL-CAI models were less harmful at a given level of helpfulness.

The limit is the analogy's own: a written policy scales enforcement, not judgment. A vague or wrong clause gets applied just as consistently as a good one — the loop makes your values legible and cheap to apply, and leaves getting them right entirely on you.

Build it yourself

build it: $1 · one evening

  1. read

    Read the paper — the 16 principles in the appendix are literally readable sentences ('choose the response that is less harmful...'); pick one and argue with it.

    1–2 hrs · $0

  2. run

    Write your own 5-principle constitution and run the CAI loop by hand via API: the model answers a red-team prompt, critiques its own answer against your principles, revises — compare before and after.

    one evening · ~$1

  3. train

    Run the full open recipe: generate an AI-feedback preference dataset from a constitution and DPO-train Mistral-7B on it — Hugging Face's result resisted jailbreaks better without losing helpfulness.

    a weekend · $20–40
