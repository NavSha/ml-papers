DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning

DeepSeek-AI · arXiv 2025 · arXiv 2501.12948

Reward exactly one thing — 'the final answer is correct' — and competition-math scores climb from 15.6% to 71.0% with zero human reasoning examples. The polished model matched OpenAI's o1 and shipped its weights.

the aha · the RL loop and the AIME climb, redrawn

Reward the answer, get the reasoning

The loop pays for exactly two things: the final answer checks out, and the work stays inside <think> tags. Nobody writes example solutions; no learned model scores the style. Run it long enough on a base model and long chains of thought, self-verification, and "aha-moment" backtracking emerge — they win, so they survive. R1-Zero's AIME pass@1 climbs from 15.6% to 71.0% with zero supervised reasoning data; the polished R1 reaches 79.8%, a hair past o1's 79.2% — with open weights. It's learning chess from nothing but win/loss feedback instead of annotated grandmaster games: strategy and self-correction emerge because they win, not because anyone demonstrated them.

Reward one thing

The loop is almost insultingly simple: the model answers, a rule checks whether the final answer is correct and formatted, that verdict is the reward, the weights update. No human-written reasoning traces anywhere — just a referee saying right or wrong.

The line climbs

Watch R1-Zero's competition-math score go from 15.6% to 71.0% with zero reasoning examples. Long chains, self-checking, and backtracking are never taught — they emerge, because they win. The curve is traced from the paper's own training run.

The polished R1 reaches 79.8% on AIME, edging OpenAI's o1 at 79.2% — and the weights shipped open. A closed frontier capability, reproduced and given away, within months.

The moral for builders

If your domain has an automatic checker — tests pass, answer matches, constraint satisfied — it is a candidate for this exact loop, and models will keep getting sharply better at it. The moat was months, not years; assume any closed capability gets replicated openly.

what it changed

If it's checkable, it's trainable

Post-training is where differentiation lives now, and its key input is a verifiable reward. If your domain has an automatic success check — tests pass, answer matches, constraint satisfied — it is a candidate for RL-style improvement, and models keep getting sharply better at it; where quality is a matter of taste, expect slower gains. Two market facts follow: R1's distilled small models put strong reasoning at commodity prices, and the open release repriced the entire market in a week. Assume any closed capability gets replicated openly within months.

honest limits

Not RL alone — and not a learned judge

The misconception: "R1 proves you only need RL — no human data." R1-Zero, the pure-RL model, had poor readability and mixed languages mid-solution; the R1 that actually shipped adds cold-start SFT data and multi-stage training. Pure RL found the capability; supervised data made it legible.

And the reward is not a learned model scoring vibes — it's a rule that checks the final answer, which is precisely why it works: a rule checker is far harder to reward-hack than a judge. That is also the method's boundary. Chess has an automatic referee, and so do math and passing tests; where success is a matter of taste, there is nothing for the loop to check.
