Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models

Wei et al., Google Brain · NeurIPS 2022 · arXiv 2201.11903

Same model, same weights: show it worked examples with the steps written out and its math-word-problem score triples — 17.9% to 56.9%, from a prompt change you could type yourself.

the aha · the before/after bar, redrawn

Only the prompt changed

figure 1 · the exemplar

Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of tennis balls. Each can has 3 tennis balls. How many tennis balls does he have now?

A: Roger started with 5 balls. 2 cans of 3 tennis balls each is 6 tennis balls. 5 + 6 = 11. The answer is 11.

The paper's exemplar, verbatim. In the standard condition the same question is answered with just "The answer is 11." — writing out the middle is the entire intervention.

That prompt change is the whole method — no fine-tuning, no new model. Paste a few worked examples like the one above in front of the question, and PaLM's solve rate on grade-school math word problems goes from 17.9% to 56.9% — a change you could type yourself. It's the difference between demanding an instant answer to a multi-digit multiplication and handing someone scratch paper. The scratch paper doesn't make anyone smarter — it externalizes intermediate state, so errors stop compounding.

what it changed

Answer quality is a dial

More reasoning tokens buy better answers — at higher latency and a bigger bill. That trade is a runtime dial now: every reasoning-effort setting and extended-thinking toggle you flip today descends from this observation. Product design follows directly — route easy queries to a fast, cheap path, spend tokens only on the hard ones, and meter the difference. With chain-of-thought, quality stops being a model property and becomes a line item you can read off the invoice.

honest limits

Scratch paper, not introspection

The misconception: "the printed reasoning is a faithful transcript of how the model computed its answer." It isn't guaranteed to be. The chain correlates with accuracy but can be post-hoc rationalization — treat it as useful scaffolding and a debugging signal, not ground-truth introspection. And this was a discovered prompting behavior: models weren't trained to reason step-by-step until years later.

The second limit saves you money: the ability is emergent, appearing only around ~100B parameters. Below that, chain-of-thought prompting can make results worse, not better — scratch paper only helps someone who already knows how to multiply; a small model just scribbles fluent nonsense.

Build it yourself

build it: $5 · one evening

  1. read

    Read the paper — the Roger-and-his-tennis-balls exemplar is the entire technique, verbatim, and the appendix carries full outputs for both prompting conditions.

    1–2 hrs · $0

  2. run

    Reproduce the headline with a cost meter attached: run 50 GSM8K problems in three conditions (direct / 'think step by step' / few-shot CoT), tracking accuracy AND tokens per condition — ~30 lines of Python, and 'buy quality with tokens' becomes a number instead of a slogan.

    one evening · <$5

Prompting could unlock reasoning, but the raw model underneath still wasn't trying to help anyone. Turning a predictor into an assistant took three training steps and a team of about forty labelers.
